Here is how you can deliver the exact same 113 pages of content without making them read a textbook:

1. The "Duolingo for Deconditioning" (Micro-Swiping)
Turn the 113 pages into 300 beautified "Truth Cards" (like Tinder swipes).

The UI: It asks: "Pornography acts as a stress-reliever because of the dopamine release."
The Action: Swipe Right (True) or Left (False).
The Hook: If they swipe Right, the card flips over with a snappy 3-sentence scientific reality check explaining why they are wrong. It forces active commitment to a belief, generating massive "Aha!" moments.
2. The Socratic "Truth Engine" Dialogues
The user never reads a chapter. The entire method is delivered via a conversational AI chat logic tree.

The Flow: The app texts: "When you are bored, do you usually watch it?" -> User: "Yes." -> App: "If you eat a sandwich when you're bored, are you less bored, or just full?"
The Hook: "People rarely argue with their own conclusions." When they answer the questions, they accidentally dismantle their own illusions.
3. The "Emergency Broadcast" (Spatial Audio Escapes)
A high-production-value, guided immersive audio path (similar to Headspace or Waking Up).

The Hook: Addicted brains have fried dopamine receptors and can't focus their eyes on text for more than 30 seconds. Audio bypasses visual fatigue. We use spatial audio to make the "urges" sound like they are coming from outside their head, reinforcing the idea that the urge is just a mechanical glitch, not them.
4. The Interactive Deduction Board (Detective Mode)
The app treats the user's addiction as an unsolved puzzle.

The Hook: The user is presented with an empty digital "evidence board." As they complete 2-minute audio clips, they earn "Pins" (Concept Tokens like "Dopamine", "The Big Monster"). They must manually drag the pins to connect the dots on their board to logically solve the puzzle.
5. The Scenario Simulator
Instead of Chapters, the content is mapped to 2D real-world scenarios.

The Hook: "It's Friday night. Your brain says 'Just a quick peek to relax.' What is your excuse?" They tap "I deserve a reward," and the app launches the brutal EasyPeasy logic deconstructing that specific fallacy. It makes the theory immediately applicable to their bedroom at 11 PM.
