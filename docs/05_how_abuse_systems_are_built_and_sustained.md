# How Systems of Abuse Are Built and Sustained

> *"Abuse is not a loss of control. It is an exertion of control using tactics that work."*
> — Lundy Bancroft, *Why Does He Do That?*

> *"Coercive control is not one act but a pattern — a liberty crime that takes away the victim's freedom and creates an unequal relationship."*
> — Evan Stark, *Coercive Control*

---

## Introduction: The Myth That Must Die First

Before anything else in this document can be understood, one central myth must be dismantled:

**"He loses control."**

This is the explanation most commonly offered for why abusive men do what they do. It is the language of legal defenses ("crime of passion"), of community apology ("he has anger issues"), of family protection ("he doesn't mean it, he just snaps"), of romantic minimization ("his love is so intense it overwhelms him").

It is a lie.

Not a simple misunderstanding. Not a partial truth. A functional lie — one that serves specific interests, that allows specific things to continue, and that costs specific people their safety, freedom, and sometimes lives.

The evidence against the "loss of control" narrative is not ambiguous:

**He does not lose control with his boss.** Even when his boss does something far more genuinely frustrating than anything she has done, he does not assault his boss. He does not flip tables in business meetings. He constrains himself precisely, consistently, without apparent difficulty.

**He does not destroy his own property.** Abusers who "lose control" in rage systematically report destroying her belongings, not their own. This targeting requires perception of property and calculation of whose loss produces the desired effect.

**He modulates the violence.** Studies of abusers document patterns of violence that carefully avoid areas likely to produce visible evidence — the body, not the face; the torso, not the arm. This requires awareness, calculation, and control throughout the act.

**He stops before killing.** Usually. The restraint required to hurt without killing is demonstrably not absent — the act of stopping demonstrates regulatory capacity.

**He explains it afterward calmly.** He justifies, minimizes, and narrativizes the incident with coherent language. This requires intact executive function. Executive function does not survive loss of control.

Beyond these observations, researcher Evan Stark's foundational work on coercive control and Donald Dutton's decades of work with abusive men both document that violence in abusive relationships is a *tactic* — selected because it works, modulated based on context, and exercised with more selectivity than the "loss of control" narrative can accommodate.

Understanding abuse as **control** — not emotion — changes everything. It changes what we look for, what we explain, what we criminalize, what we believe, and what we demand.

## Part One: The Architecture of Coercive Control — Six Mechanisms

Evan Stark's coercive control framework, first published in 2007 and now criminalized in the UK, Scotland, Ireland, and parts of Australia, provides the most comprehensive analytical map of what intimate partner abuse actually is.

Stark's argument: **physical violence is not the defining feature of abuse**. Physical violence is one tactic within a broader pattern of control that colonizes every aspect of a victim's life. The pattern — not the isolated incident — is the true violence.

The six mechanisms of coercive control operate together and reinforce each other. None is typically deployed alone. Their combined effect is the systematic dismantling of the victim's liberty.

---

### Mechanism 1: Isolation — Severing All Connections

**What it is**:

Isolation is the systematic severance of the victim's connections to the world outside the abuser — to friends, family, colleagues, communities, and support systems. It is the first step in establishing a closed system in which the abuser's reality becomes the only reality, and the only person with access to both pain and relief is the abuser.

**How it is accomplished**:

Isolation rarely arrives as an explicit prohibition ("You cannot see your friends"). It arrives as:

*Criticism and conflict creation*: Friends are gradually characterized as: bad for her, jealous of the relationship, interested in her sexually (possessive jealousy), having bad values, incompatible with who she is now. Family members are characterized as: controlling, not understanding of the relationship, undermining their bond. Each criticism creates friction around the connection until she chooses between the friendship and peace at home, and the value of peace at home has been artificially inflated by the threat of what happens when peace is absent.

*Time monopolization*: Demanding her time and presence in ways that make maintaining other relationships practically impossible. Getting upset when she makes plans without him. Creating crises that require her presence on evenings when she had social plans.

*Geographic relocation*: Convincing or pressuring her to move — for his job, for a "fresh start," for a cheaper living situation — that places her at a distance from her existing support network. Once relocated, the social network must be rebuilt from scratch in an environment he controls.

*Communication monitoring*: Reading texts, emails, and social media messages. Demanding access to passwords. Installing location-tracking software. Creating an environment in which all communications happen in awareness of his potential surveillance.

*Creating financial dependency*: If she cannot independently access money, she cannot independently travel, make plans, or ultimately leave.

*Weaponizing her disclosures*: If she has disclosed private information about her family or friends — as people do in intimate relationships — he may use this information to create conflict between her and those people, or to convince her that talking to them is risky because "they'll use it against her."

**The effect**:

She has no one to tell. She has no one to validate her reality. She has no one who has been watching long enough to see the pattern. She is alone in a reality he constructs.

The critical clinical consequence: **social isolation is one of the strongest predictors of severity of outcome in intimate partner violence**. When she has active support networks, the trajectory of abuse is shorter and outcomes are better. Isolation is not a side effect of abuse — it is a tactical prerequisite for its continuation.

**The neuropsychological dimension**:

Social connection is a biological regulatory resource. Humans co-regulate their nervous systems through connection — the presence of a safe other literally calters the physiological stress response. When Stark writes that coercive control is a "liberty crime," he means that it deprives the victim of the social fabric through which humans maintain psychological integrity.

Without access to co-regulation, under chronic stress, in an environment that is both the source of threat and the only available refuge, the victim's nervous system has nowhere to settle. Everything described in the trauma neuroscience of Document 01 proceeds from this foundational isolation.

### Mechanism 2: Monopolization of Perception — Becoming the Only Reality

**What it is**:

Once isolated from external reality checks, the abuser positions himself as the primary — eventually sole — interpreter of reality, of her, of the relationship, and of the external world.

This is **gaslighting** taken to a systemic level — not individual incidents of denied reality but a sustained, structured campaign to replace her perception with his.

**How it operates**:

*Memory revision*: "That didn't happen." "You imagined that." "You always exaggerate." Applied consistently over time to her accounts of incidents, her memories of conversations, her sense of what was said or promised, this undermines the basic reliability of her own memory as an instrument for knowing the world.

*Perception correction*: "You're too sensitive." "You misunderstand things." "You read situations wrong." "Other people don't react the way you do." Applied to her emotional responses and social perceptions, this undermines the reliability of her emotional intelligence and social cognition.

*Reality monopoly*: Because he has isolated her from external validators, there is no one whose perception she can compare to her own. His interpretation of the situation is the only competing interpretation she has regular access to. The echo chamber becomes total.

*Information control*: In some coercive control situations, explicit information control is exercised — restrictions on news consumption, on social media, on contact with politically or ideologically different perspectives. In less extreme versions, he simply becomes the primary frame through which she interprets all incoming information.

*Pathologizing*: A particularly powerful variant involves framing her resistance to his reality as evidence of a mental health problem ("You're crazy," "You're paranoid," "You need help"). If she has had mental health struggles — particularly if he knows about them — these are weaponized as explanations for her "wrong" perception of the situation.

**The psychological effect**:

The epistemological foundation of selfhood — the reliable sense that I can trust my own perceptions, memories, and emotional responses to know something about reality — is attacked at its root.

When this attack succeeds — and over prolonged periods, without external validators, it tends to — she loses access to her own reality. She defers to his. Not because she is stupid or particularly vulnerable. Because the human mind, deprived of external reality checks and subjected to consistent pressure that her own perceptions are wrong, cannot maintain certainty about its own reliability indefinitely.

This is not a personality defect in the victim. This is the predictable outcome of a sophisticated campaign against a fundamental human vulnerability: our dependence on social validation of our perceptions.

**Why it matters for understanding survivor behavior**:

When a survivor denies that something happened, defends the abuser's version of events, minimizes the severity of what occurred, or seems to have an inconsistent or self-contradictory account — this is often the product of Mechanism 2. She has been systematically disconnected from her own reliable perception of events. Her account reflects not clarity but the damage done to her perceptual access.

This is one of the most important things for anyone in contact with survivors — clinicians, investigators, family, friends — to understand.

### Mechanism 3: Degradation — The Systematic Dismantling of Selfhood

**What it is**:

Degradation is the sustained attack on the victim's sense of worth, competence, attractiveness, intelligence, and moral character. It functions by occupying and colonizing her self-concept — replacing her own assessments of herself with his, and making his assessments the mirror through which she sees herself.

When degradation succeeds, she no longer needs him to tell her she is worthless. She already knows it. He has internalized himself into her self-perception.

**The forms it takes**:

*Verbal humiliation*: Name-calling, contempt, disgust, contemptuous laughter at her ideas, her appearance, her responses, her competence. "You're stupid." "Look at yourself." "No one else would put up with you." "You're disgusting."

*Public degradation*: Humiliating her in front of others — making her the subject of jokes, correcting her in public, dismissing her contributions in social contexts, treating her as less than. This serves a dual function: it degrades her, and it demonstrates to potential helpers that they too should regard her as he does.

*Sexual humiliation*: Degradation in sexual contexts — commenting on her body with contempt, performing sexual acts that she finds humiliating and treating her discomfort as amusing or arousing, recording her without consent, comparing her unfavorably to others, using sexual performance as a site for enacting contempt.

*Competence attacks*: Consistently positioning her as incompetent in areas that matter to her — her parenting, her work, her social relationships, her reasoning. Each criticism, delivered with enough certainty and backed by enough power, lodges itself in her self-assessment.

**The progressive internalization**:

The most powerful form of degradation is when the target internalizes the abuser's voice as her own internal narrator. This is the C-PTSD negative self-concept described in Document 03, operating through a specific mechanism: she has absorbed him.

When she subsequently walks into a room and immediately registers herself as the least worth noticing, the most likely to embarrass, the most in danger of being rejected — she is often not in a realistic assessment of her actual social value. She is experiencing the internalized voice of someone who had a specific interest in her believing this.

### Mechanism 4: Microregulation — The Elimination of Agency

**What it is**:

Microregulation is the extension of control into the details of daily life — dictating not just the broad outlines of the relationship but the specific, moment-by-moment conditions of her existence. It is the expression of control as management: of her time, her appearance, her movements, her relationships, her work, her domestic practice, her consumption.

**How it operates**:

*Appearance control*: What she wears, how she presents, whether makeup is required or forbidden, whether she is permitted to dress in ways that other men might find attractive. This control is often framed in the language of care and jealousy ("I just don't want other men looking at you") but is fundamentally control over her bodily presentation as extension of his property.

*Movement control*: Where she goes, when, with whom, for how long. Requiring check-in calls. Questioning or punishing time that cannot be fully accounted for. This is often enabled by location-tracking technology.

*Domestic regulation*: Rules about how the house must be maintained — cleaning standards, food standards, organizational standards — and consequences (emotional, verbal, or physical) for failure to maintain them. The home as a site of constant evaluation and potential failure.

*Financial control*: As discussed under isolation — control of money produces dependency that makes everything else harder to resist.

*Parenting regulation*: Controlling her relationship with children — undermining her parenting, contradicting her with children, using children as messengers or informants, restricting or weaponizing access to children.

**The effect on agency**:

Agency — the capacity to make choices and enact them — is like a muscle. It requires use to maintain. When the environment systematically removes the opportunity for choice — when all significant decisions are made by the abuser, when her preferences are irrelevant or dangerous, when expressing a desire produces conflict — the agency-muscle atrophies.

This atrophy is precisely what many outside observers notice when they wonder why survivors don't leave more decisively. The capacity for decisive, self-determined action requires a self that has been maintained through the practice of agency. Prolonged microregulation removes that practice. The self can no longer locate its own desires clearly, cannot confidently enumerate options, cannot sustain the executive function necessary for planning and executing departure.

**The therapy implication**:

Recovery from prolonged microregulation requires specific work on rebuilding agency — not through demands for decisive action, but through slowly practicing choice in small, safe domains. Choosing what to eat, what to wear, how to spend an hour — these feel trivial to someone whose agency is intact. After prolonged microregulation, they are substantial.

### Mechanism 5: Creating a Climate of Fear — Violence as Policy

**What it is**:

Not all coercive control involves direct physical violence. In many documented cases, the climate of fear — the constant, low-level awareness that violence is available and might be deployed — is sufficient to produce compliance without its regular physical enactment.

Fear is the most energy-efficient control mechanism available. Once established, it operates automatically. He doesn't need to hit her every day if she remembers, every day, that he could.

**How the climate is created and maintained**:

*Violence that leaves no marks*: Hair pulling, choking, grabs, twists, pressure — acts that hurt, that demonstrate capacity, that control, but that are unlikely to produce visible evidence.

*Property violence*: Objects thrown through the room (not at her — near her). Doors punched. Items belonging to her or others destroyed. These demonstrations calibrate the threat precisely: he is capable of this level of force, and it could shift from property to person.

*Animals and children as threat vehicles*: Harming, threatening, or demonstrating control over pets or children — beings she is attached to and responsible for — as an extension of the threat toward her. "Your dog might have an accident if you go out tonight." This is documented in coercive control research and survivor accounts.

*Verbal escalation*: Descriptions of what he could do. What he has done to others. "You don't know what I'm capable of." "You don't want to find out what happens when you push me." These are not idle threats. They are calibrated communications that he has monitored her response to and knows to be effective.

*Surveillance*: The knowledge that she is being watched — that her movements, communications, and relationships are monitored — produces a continuous low-level fear response. She cannot let her guard down because she cannot know when she is not being observed.

**The neurological cost**:

Living under a sustained climate of fear is neurologically equivalent to living in a combat zone. The HPA axis runs in a state of chronic low-level activation. The amygdala scans continuously for threat cues. The prefrontal cortex — engaged in ongoing threat assessment — has reduced capacity for other functions: planning, creativity, pleasure, cognitive flexibility.

Survivors of prolonged coercive control often demonstrate cognitive and emotional profiles that are neurologically indistinguishable from combat veterans with PTSD. They are, in the most precise sense, veterans of a prolonged war — conducted in the ostensibly private domain of their own home.

### Mechanism 6: Sexual Coercion — The Weaponization of Intimacy

**What it is**:

Sexual coercion within relationships is the use of sex as a site and instrument of control — through demands, through consequences for refusal, through the performance of acts that humiliate or harm, through recording without consent, through reproductive coercion.

It is the area where the control discussed in this document and the harm discussed in Document 02 most directly intersect.

**How it operates**:

*Sexual entitlement*: Sex is demanded as a right of the relationship. Refusal is not accepted as a valid response but as a provocation, a betrayal, or evidence of infidelity or lack of love. This demand is often structured through coercion (implicit or explicit consequences for refusal) rather than through force, particularly in the early stages of the relationship.

*Coercive escalation*: Sexual acts are escalated over time — more painful, more degrading, more extreme — with each new step normalized by the prior one. What would have been clearly unacceptable at the beginning of the relationship has become, through gradual escalation, the current norm. This escalation is rarely accidental. It tests her compliance and expands the range of what her compliance permits.

*Weaponizing her trauma history*: If she has disclosed prior sexual trauma — which intimate relationships often involve — this may be used against her: as a reason she "needs" particular acts ("you're so damaged"), as leverage ("I'm the only one who accepts you as you are"), or as evidence that her objections to current acts are "just her issues."

*Non-consensual recording*: Photographing or recording sexual encounters without consent — a form of assault — which then becomes a tool of control: "If you leave, I'll send these to your employer / parents / friends."

*Reproductive coercion*: Sabotaging contraception (adding holes to condoms, removing contraception, lying about one's own reproductive health), pressuring or coercing pregnancy, preventing access to reproductive health care. Pregnancy as a mechanism for deepening the economic, practical, and legal entanglement that makes departure harder.

**The specific damage of sexual coercion within relationship**:

Beyond the harms documented in Documents 01 and 02, sexual coercion within intimate relationship produces a specific dimension of damage: the contamination of intimacy itself.

Intimacy — the capacity to be close, to be vulnerable, to open the body and the emotional self to another person — requires the experience of safety in that domain. When intimacy has been the site through which harm was delivered, the experience of closeness no longer signals safety. It signals threat.

For survivors of sexual coercion within relationships, subsequent intimacy — even with safe partners who are genuinely gentle and respectful — triggers the threat-response. The body reads "closeness" as "incoming harm" because that is what closeness meant for a significant period of experience.

Rewiring this association — helping the body learn that closeness can be safe — is one of the most demanding and time-consuming aspects of recovery from intimate partner sexual abuse. It cannot be rushed. It cannot be demanded. It can only be slowly learned in repeated, patient, fully voluntary encounters whose character is categorically different from what was learned.

## Part Two: The Real Answer to "Why She Didn't Leave" — 20 Reasons

This question — asked with genuine puzzlement, sometimes with skepticism, sometimes with accusation — deserves the comprehensive answer it has never consistently received.

Not one or two reasons. All of them.

**1. Because leaving is statistically the most dangerous moment.**
Intimate partner violence escalates to homicide at highest rates during the period immediately following or around separation. Glass et al. (2008) documented 7.48x increased homicide risk in the period following separation attempts. Campbell et al. (2003) confirmed that femicide risk is concentrated in the separation period. She is not irrationally afraid. She is correctly assessing a documented risk.

**2. Because he told her he would kill her.**
Most intimate partner homicide perpetrators have made explicit prior threats. She heard them. She believed them because she had already seen his capacity for violence.

**3. Because she has no money.**
Financial abuse affects 98% of domestic violence cases (National DV Hotline). Without money, she cannot pay a deposit on an apartment, cannot travel, cannot afford the legal fees for a protective order. Without money, departure is logistically impossible.

**4. Because she has nowhere to go.**
Domestic violence shelters in the United States turn away more than 10,000 requests for shelter on any given day due to capacity limitations (NNEDV, 2019). The infrastructure to support survivors is catastrophically underfunded relative to need.

**5. Because her support network has been destroyed.**
After months or years of isolation (Mechanism 1), the friends and family who might have helped have been systematically pushed away. She has no one to call who knows what is happening.

**6. Because she has children.**
Leaving with children immediately creates legal complexity around custody. Courts' tendency to insist on continued father access means she may face unsupervised visits she cannot protect her children from. He may threaten to take the children, and courts have been slow to protect children from abusive fathers in custody cases.

**7. Because she loves him.**
This is real. The love and the abuse are not mutually exclusive. She loves a person who is also destructive. Trauma bonding (Document 03) means the relationship carries a neurochemical intensity that real feeling coexists uncomfortably with.

**8. Because he is also sometimes loving.**
The reconciliation phase of the cycle is real — the tenderness, the apologies, the man she fell in love with returning. She stays for that person because that person is also real, not just a manipulation.

**9. Because she believes he will change.**
He has promised. The promises are believable because they are sometimes accompanied by weeks of genuine change. The cycle resets. The hope is sustainable often enough to sustain it.

**10. Because she blames herself.**
If she caused it — by fighting back, by saying the wrong thing, by not managing his moods correctly — then she can also prevent it. Self-blame produces a sense of agency that makes staying feel like strategy rather than trap.

**11. Because she doesn't know what happened to her has a name.**
Many survivors, particularly those whose abuse was largely non-physical, do not recognize their experience as "domestic abuse." They know something is wrong. They don't have a framework that allows them to claim the seriousness of the label.

**12. Because she is ashamed.**
Because he told her no one would believe her. Because she believes the degradation she was subjected to — that she is worthless, undeserving, the cause of what happened. Shame keeps people in places they don't deserve to be in.

**13. Because immigration status.**
Many survivors have precarious immigration status, and he controls that status — directly (sponsorship of visa) or through threats to report her. Departure means either catastrophic legal jeopardy or catastrophic loss.

**14. Because her children love him.**
Even when she is clear about her own experience, separating her children from a father they love is a painful decision she may not be able to bring herself to make — even knowing what she knows.

**15. Because the legal system has historically failed survivors.**
She may know — from her own community, from others she knows, from public discourse — how unlikely justice is. A process that will require her to re-narrate her trauma to skeptical strangers with a 95%+ chance of no conviction and a near-certainty of retraumatization seems like it costs more than it offers.

**16. Because he threatened suicide.**
If you leave me, I'll kill myself. This is a documented coercive tactic. For a person who genuinely cares about the abuser — which she often does — this is a crushing weight. His death would be her fault. She cannot carry that, and he knows it.

**17. Because she has been told, by her community, to stay.**
Family, religious community, cultural tradition: "You made a commitment." "Think of the children." "Work it out." "Marriage is hard." These messages, from people she trusts, reinforce staying as the moral position.

**18. Because the abuse wasn't "bad enough."**
Internalized minimization — from comparison to cases that were "worse," from his minimization of what occurred, from a culture that reserves the "abuse" label for visibly extreme cases — produces a survivor who doubts her right to leave because the situation doesn't meet the threshold she has been given.

**19. Because leaving is a process, not a moment.**
Research on departure from abusive relationships (Langer, Walker, Campbell) documents that survivors on average leave and return multiple times before leaving permanently. Each departure produces information, resources, and learning that informs the next one. The process is not linear but it is movement. Returning is not failure. It is often a necessary step in the process.

**20. Because she is exhausted.**
The cognitive load of living under coercive control — the continuous threat assessment, the emotional management of the abuser's moods, the monitoring of every word and action for its potential to trigger violence, the management of children and household in this environment — produces a chronic exhaustion that leaves little capacity for the enormous, dangerous, complex, logistically demanding project of departure.

## Part Three: Institutional Systems That Enable Abuse

Coercive control does not only exist in private space. It is sustained by public systems that were designed — in many cases explicitly — to manage rather than prevent it, and that continue to fail survivors in ways that are documented, measurable, and persistent.

### The Legal System

**Historical framework**: Marital rape was not a crime in all US states until 1993. Before this, the law explicitly endorsed the abuser's claim to a wife's body. In many parts of the world, equivalent legal protections remain absent or weakly enforced.

**Evidentiary standards**: Sexual assault law requires a level of evidence not typically required for other violent crimes. The victim's credibility is a principal line of defense — her sexual history, her prior relationship to the defendant, her demeanor during testimony. This is the product of a legal tradition in which women's testimony was structurally less credible than men's.

**Conviction statistics**: UK Government statistics (2021) indicate that approximately 1.6% of reported rapes result in a conviction in England and Wales. US estimates are similarly discouraging. Low conviction rates reflect both the evidentiary burden and implicit bias in jury decision-making — and they are known to survivors, who calculate them into decisions about whether to report.

**Protective order failures**: Protective orders (restraining orders) are commonly violated. Law enforcement response to violations has been historically inadequate. In the US, the Supreme Court decision *Castle Rock v. Gonzales* (2005) established that police are not constitutionally required to enforce protective orders — a legal framework that leaves survivors who had relied on those orders in extreme danger.

**Custody law**: Family courts' inclination toward contact between children and fathers, even in the context of documented domestic violence, effectively extends the abuser's access and control over the survivor through the children, often for years after separation.

**Immigration law**: As noted above, immigration status weaponized within a relationship becomes a legal structure that enables abuse.

### The Medical System

**Historical diagnosis**: "Hysteria" — a diagnosis applied almost exclusively to women — pathologized the symptoms of unacknowledged trauma across a century of medical practice. Symptoms we now recognize as PTSD, C-PTSD, and somatic trauma responses were attributed to inherent female irrationality.

**Contemporary misdiagnosis**: Somatic symptoms of trauma (fibromyalgia, IBS, chronic fatigue, pelvic pain) are still frequently treated in isolation from their traumatic etiology, resulting in symptom management rather than trauma treatment.

**Insufficient trauma screening**: Emergency departments, primary care practices, and OB-GYN offices represent potential points of contact with survivors — yet remain inconsistent in universal trauma screening and in trauma-informed response when disclosure occurs.

**Non-consensual examination practices**: Forensic rape examinations — necessary for evidence collection — can be deeply traumatizing. The "rape kit" examination involves physical examination of the site of violation, in a clinical environment, often by strangers, immediately after the assault. Trauma-informed practices for forensic examination exist but remain inconsistently implemented.

### The Cultural System

**Romantic coercion tropes**: The narrative of romantic persistence as charming despite repeated refusal — a mainstay of Hollywood romantic comedy and drama — normalizes coercive pursuit as evidence of love. The man who won't take no for an answer is romantic. The woman who says no and means it is a challenge to be overcome.

Research has documented correlations between exposure to romantic media that normalizes coercion and acceptance of rape myths and coercive behavior in real relationships.

**Music**: Lyrical content of popular music has been analyzed extensively (Adams & Fuller, 2006; Weitzer & Kubrin, 2009) for misogynistic themes that range from dehumanization of women to explicit glorification of sexual violence. This content is absorbed by young people — particularly adolescents — during the period when sexual and gender attitudes are being formed.

**Online culture**: Incel communities, "red-pill" movements, and pickup artist culture have developed sophisticated ideological frameworks that justify male sexual entitlement, attribute female non-compliance to manipulation, and construct sexual violence as a corrective to unjust female sexual power. These frameworks radicalize isolated young men and provide community support for attitudes that facilitate abuse.

### The Economic System

**The wage gap**: Women earn approximately 82 cents to the dollar earned by men (US, 2021). This structural gap, compounded by career interruption penalties for caregiving, produces the economic dependency that makes leaving abusive relationships logistically difficult for millions of women.

**Inadequate paid leave**: Surviving abuse often requires time off work — for legal processes, medical appointments, housing searches, childcare gaps created by the departure. Most low-wage workers in the US have no paid leave. Leaving an abuser may cost a job, which may close off departure.

**Housing costs**: In most US cities, the cost of independent housing consumes a majority of a low-wage worker's income. For women whose earning capacity is lower by structural design, independent housing is simply unaffordable without financial assistance that shelters cannot consistently provide.

## Part Four: What Coercive Control Criminalization Looks Like

The UK Serious Crime Act (2015) criminalized coercive and controlling behavior in intimate and familial relationships — a landmark in recognizing that the pattern, not just isolated incidents, constitutes the crime.

The law provides:
- Criminal charge for a pattern of behavior that has a serious effect on the victim
- Definition of "serious effect" as causing the victim to fear violence on at least two occasions or causing serious alarm/distress that has a substantial adverse effect on daily activities
- Maximum sentence of 5 years imprisonment

Scotland, Ireland, France, and parts of Australia have enacted equivalent legislation.

The United States has not enacted a federal coercive control statute, though several states have in various forms. Many US advocates argue this gap leaves large populations of survivors without legal recognition of the full nature of what they experienced.

**Why criminalization matters beyond punishment**:

When the pattern, not just the incident, is the crime, several things change:
- Survivors' accounts of ongoing patterns — rather than specific isolated incidents they may not be able to fully document — become legally relevant
- The tactical, deliberate nature of abuse is legally named
- Earlier intervention becomes possible — before the violence required to prove "assault" has occurred
- The cultural recognition of coercive control as serious harm increases awareness that may support survivors in earlier identification of their situation

## Part Five: Male Perpetrators — Who They Are and What They Believe

Understanding the perpetrators — not to excuse them but to model them accurately — is necessary for effective prevention and intervention.

### The Profile

Perpetrators of intimate partner abuse are not primarily:
- People with diagnosable personality disorders (while antisocial personality disorder and narcissistic personality disorder are correlated, most perpetrators do not meet diagnostic criteria)
- People who lose control (as established in the introduction)
- People who are inherently malevolent

They are primarily:
- People who believe they are entitled to particular treatment from a partner
- People who have learned — from family modeling, from peer culture, from ideology — that the tactics of coercive control are legitimate responses to perceived violations of entitlement
- People who do not experience themselves as abusive, because "abusive" does not fit their self-concept
- People who have a specific and functional set of beliefs about women, relationships, and male prerogative

David Lisak's research on "undetected rapists" — men who described sexual assault in interview but did not label it as such — documented specific belief profiles:
- Belief that women use sexual reluctance as manipulation
- Belief that male persistence is legitimate and eventual compliance is genuine
- Limited acknowledgment of victim impact
- Entitlement orientation toward female sexuality

Lundy Bancroft's *Why Does He Do That?* documents similar findings in his clinical work with abusive men: **abusive behavior is not emotion-driven. It is attitude-driven.** The rages, the jealousy, the possessiveness — these are expressions of attitudes about relationship, about women, and about entitlement that are culturally sourced and deliberately maintained.

This is why anger management — the most commonly assigned intervention for domestic violence — has consistently failed to reduce recidivism. Anger management addresses emotion, not attitude. The technique that has the strongest evidence base, Duluth Model batterer intervention programs focused on challenging entitlement beliefs, is still underfunded and underimplemented.

## Conclusion: The System Can Change But Only If Named

Abuse is not a private matter between two individuals. It is a pattern that is enabled, sustained, and sometimes licensed by systems — legal, medical, economic, cultural.

The individual work — the courage of every survivor who leaves, every intervention by a bystander who names what they see — happens within a system that either supports or undermines it. Changing outcomes requires changing both.

This requires:
- Legal recognition of coercive control as a crime
- Adequate funding of survivor infrastructure (shelters, legal aid, financial support)
- Medical training in trauma-informed care and domestic violence recognition
- Educational investment in healthy relationship modeling from early childhood
- Cultural change in the narratives about love, persistence, and entitlement that are reproduced across media
- Communities willing to hold perpetrators accountable rather than protecting their reputations

None of this is simple. All of it is necessary.
